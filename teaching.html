We rarely speak with complete specificity about what we want, what is required, or what is allowed. For example, I might say ‘I want ice cream,’ and see no need to specify that certain kinds of ice cream, e.g. melted, or toxic ice cream, would not satisfy my desire. Indeed, being completely specific about what I want is practically impossible. For in addition to being non-melted and non-toxic, the desire I would ordinarily describe with ‘I want ice cream’ would be for ice cream that contains no dirt, no petroleum, and so on. Being completely specific, it seems, would require explicitly ruling out an infinite number of possibilities. This gives rise to a puzzle: if speakers almost always underspecify their desires, as in this example, how do hearers manage to successfully understand what they want?

In my research, I draw on resources from philosophy, logic, linguistics, and computer science, in order to give a formal account of the conventions and contextual factors that determine what underspecific modal claims mean and how they are interpreted. In my dissertation, Underspecific Modals, I develop a semantic theory for natural language modal constructions that addresses longstanding puzzles in logic that arise for underspecific modal claims, including Ross’s Puzzle, the problem of Free Choice Permission, and the Good Samaritan paradox.
